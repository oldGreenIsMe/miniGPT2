To be, or not to be, that is the question:
Whether 'tis nobler in the mind to suffer
The slings and arrows of outrageous fortune,
Or to take arms against a sea of troubles
And by opposing end them.

When morning light appears upon the hill,
The silent birds awake and sing their song.
The earth grows warm beneath the rising sun,
And all the world resumes its ancient motion.
Men walk the roads with purpose in their hearts,
And women speak of labor, hope, and home.

The king has lost his sleep to thoughts of war.
His chamber cold, his candle burning low,
He hears the distant thunder in the night
And wonders if the storm is in the sky
Or in the restless kingdom of his mind.
No servant dares disturb his troubled hour.

A soldier stood beside the broken gate
And watched the banners tremble in the wind.
He knew that dawn would bring the sound of drums,
The cry of men, the clash of steel and fear.
Yet still he kept his duty without word,
For courage often walks with silent feet.

The scholar in his room of dust and books
Turned fragile pages with a careful hand.
He sought in ancient lines some hidden truth,
A law of stars, a measure of the soul.
But every answer opened deeper doubt,
And every doubt became another door.

What is a name, if not a breath of sound?
What is a crown, if not a ring of care?
What is a life, if not a passing flame,
That shines a little while against the dark?
The old man asked, but no reply was near,
Except the echo of his lonely voice.

Across the field the autumn grasses bent,
And evening spread its purple over stone.
A mother called her children from the path,
A shepherd gathered all his scattered flock.
The village fires awakened one by one,
Like earthly stars beneath the silent moon.

I saw a river winding through the plain,
Its surface bright beneath the noon-day sun.
It carried leaves and branches to the sea,
As time bears all things onward to their end.
No hand can hold the current in its place,
No eye can see the moment fully stay.

The merchant spoke of silver, grain, and wool,
Of ships delayed by weather on the coast.
He counted every risk, each debt, each gain,
And measured fortune like a careful scale.
Yet still he feared the turn of unseen chance,
For numbers never master all of life.

The child who sat beside the window frame
Asked why the stars were distant, cold, and bright.
The father smiled, yet could not answer well.
He told of lamps hung high by hands divine,
Of angels walking softly through the dark,
And left the greater questions to the night.

Now winter came and wrapped the earth in frost.
The trees stood bare against a field of white.
The air was sharp, the sky a sheet of glass,
The road a narrow thread through sleep and snow.
Still at the hearth the living gathered close,
And shared their bread, their silence, and their warmth.

If love be strong, it does not boast aloud.
It waits, it listens, and it learns to bear.
It does not flee when sorrow fills the room,
Nor turn away when beauty fades with time.
It keeps a patient fire through changing years,
And grows more deep where passion once was bright.

The bell was heard beyond the market square.
Its iron voice declared the coming hour.
Some hurried home, some lingered where they stood,
As if delay might soften what must come.
But time moves on with neither hate nor mercy,
And all men walk within its steady law.

I dreamed a tower standing in the mist,
Its upper stones concealed from mortal sight.
A narrow stair climbed upward without end,
And every step was worn by countless feet.
Perhaps all wisdom is such a tower,
And every learner climbs by faith alone.

So let us write, and speak, and build, and try,
Though none can claim possession of the whole.
A single mind perceives a single part,
A single age mistakes itself for all.
Yet truth may gather from our broken fragments,
As dawn is made from many fading stars.
